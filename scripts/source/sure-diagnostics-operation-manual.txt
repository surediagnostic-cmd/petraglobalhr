2.2  Equipment Management
All clinical and administrative equipment must be properly operated, maintained, and shut down daily. Equipment misuse or negligence will result in penalties.
Power Management Rules
	•	All clinical equipment (analysers, ultrasound, X-ray, digitiser) must be on UPS or inverter. If on UPS, shut down immediately when power fails, then switch to alternative source.

	•	Each piece of equipment has an individual Equipment Maintenance Log. The Head of Unit updates it after every service, calibration, or repair.

Customer Service & SurePACE
Our patient experience philosophy and service standards
3.1  The SurePACE Framework
SurePACE — Personalised Amazing Customer Experience — is the core of everything we do at Sure Diagnostics. It ensures that every patient who walks through our doors leaves satisfied, reassured, and with a smile of hope. SurePACE has four components:

Component
What It Means
1. Royal Reception
Every patient is welcomed warmly from the gate and at the reception. They are made to feel Important, Welcomed, and Loved from the first second.
2. Great Delivery
Services are delivered accurately, promptly, and professionally. The patient is kept informed and comfortable throughout.
3. Royal Departure
The patient leaves feeling satisfied, appreciated, and with clarity on next steps (results timing, follow-up, doctor referral).
4. Follow-Up
Every patient receives a follow-up call or WhatsApp message within 24–48 hours. Partners are called to check on referred patients and whether results aided management.

3.2  What We Owe Our Customers
Accuracy
Patients and partners expect reliable, trustworthy results. Every result you produce must be verified and accurate. When in doubt, seek a second opinion from a senior colleague or the Pathologist/Radiologist before release.
Availability
Patients expect us to be waiting for them — not the other way around. No patient should wait more than 30 minutes for their service to begin under normal circumstances. In emergencies, there is no waiting — prioritise immediately and inform other patients politely.
Partnership
Build professional bonds with both patients and referring partners. Call all patients within 24–48 hours of their visit to ask after their health. Call referring doctors to confirm that your results helped in patient management. This is what separates a great diagnostic centre from a good one.
Advice
Share clinically appropriate tips with different patient categories — antenatal women, diabetic patients, hypertensives. Help patients stay positive and informed. Never prescribe drugs, but guide patients back to their doctors with the information they need.

Laboratory Operations
Sample collection, processing, quality control & result release
4.1  Patient Reception in the Laboratory
The phlebotomist or lab technician must apply the same SurePACE principles as the reception desk. Every patient entering the laboratory must be greeted warmly and made to feel comfortable.
	•	Welcome the patient warmly: 'Welcome Sir/Ma.'
	•	Confirm patient identity against the request/booking form and LIS record.
	•	Confirm payment by checking the receipt copy. Collect the lab copy of the receipt.
	•	Inform the patient of the procedure, what to expect, and when results will be ready.
	•	Maintain patient privacy and confidentiality at all times.
	•	Recommend additional clinically relevant tests where appropriate — but always inform the referring partner before adding tests to referred patients.

4.2  Sample Collection Standards
All samples must be collected according to the specific protocol for each test type. 'Always follow best practices' is not sufficient — specific procedures are required. The following standards apply to all collections:
	•	Use the correct collection tube for each test (EDTA for FBC, SST/plain for biochemistry, citrate for coagulation, etc.).
	•	Label all sample tubes at the point of collection with: patient name, LIS number, date and time of collection, and type of sample.
	•	An unlabelled tube must never be processed. It is rejected immediately.
	•	Perform venepuncture using aseptic technique. Wear gloves at all times.
	•	Inform the patient of what you are doing and what to expect.
	•	Document all collected samples in the Lab Record Book before transferring to the processing area.

SHARPS SAFETY: Never re-cap needles using the two-hand technique. Use the single-hand scoop method or a safety device. Dispose of all sharps immediately after use into an approved yellow sharps bin. NEVER leave sharps on benches or in non-sharps bins.

4.3  Sharps Injury Protocol
If a sharps injury (needlestick, cut, splash) occurs, follow these steps immediately:
	•	Do not panic. Allow the wound to bleed slightly by squeezing gently under running water. Do NOT suck the wound.
	•	Wash thoroughly with soap and running water for at least 2 minutes.
	•	Report to the Branch Manager immediately. Log the incident in the Sharps Injury Register.
	•	The Branch Manager arranges baseline HIV/HBV/HCV blood testing for the injured staff member within 2 hours.
	•	The COO and HR Manager are notified. The staff member is referred to an occupational health physician for post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) assessment within 24 hours.
	•	The source patient is counselled and tested where consent is obtained.
	•	The Sharps Injury Register is reviewed monthly by the COO.

4.5  Quality Control (IQC) Procedure
Internal Quality Control (IQC) must be performed at the start of every analytical session, and after any reagent change, equipment maintenance, or power interruption. NO patient results are released until IQC passes.
	•	Use commercial control materials at two levels (normal and pathological) for every analyser.
	•	Plot control values on a Levey-Jennings chart in the IQC Log Book.
	•	Apply Westgard rules. Reject the analytical run if any of the following are violated: 1-3s (one control exceeds ±3 SD), 2-2s (two consecutive controls exceed ±2 SD), R-4s (range between two controls exceeds 4 SD), 4-1s (four consecutive controls exceed ±1 SD in same direction).
	•	On IQC failure: repeat the control run. Check reagent integrity and calibration. If failure persists, take the analyser offline and escalate to the Hub Lab Head.
	•	Document ALL IQC results (pass and fail) in the daily QC register. Incomplete QC records are a non-conformance.

4.6  External Quality Assurance (EQA)
Sure Diagnostics must be enrolled in at least one nationally or internationally recognised EQA/Proficiency Testing programme appropriate to our test menu. Recommended schemes: RECMEL (Nigeria), UKNEQAS, or a FMLS-affiliated national programme.
	•	EQA samples must be processed identically to patient samples — no special handling.
	•	EQA results are submitted by the Hub Lab Head within the scheme's deadline.
	•	EQA performance reports are reviewed at the monthly Clinical Meeting.
	•	Any unsatisfactory EQA result triggers a CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action) within 7 days.

5.3  Radiation Safety (X-Ray) — ALARA Principle
All X-ray procedures must comply with the ALARA principle: As Low As Reasonably Achievable. Every exposure must be the minimum necessary to produce a diagnostically acceptable image. These rules are mandatory and non-negotiable.

RADIATION SAFETY IS A LEGAL REQUIREMENT UNDER NAEC GUIDELINES. Violations expose both the staff member and the business to criminal liability.

	•	No person other than the patient — and an essential helper wearing a lead apron — may be in the X-ray room during an exposure. All other staff must be behind the protective barrier.
	•	The radiographer operates the X-ray machine from the control position with the protective barrier between them and the X-ray tube.
	•	Pregnant staff must not operate X-ray equipment or remain in the X-ray room during any exposure. Pregnancy must be declared to the Branch Manager immediately upon confirmation.
	•	Pregnant patients: the requesting clinician must be consulted before proceeding. X-ray is only performed where the clinical benefit outweighs the risk, and the abdomen must be shielded.
	•	All radiology staff must wear a personal dosimeter badge at all times. Badges are collected monthly and sent for reading. Any reading above the monthly limit triggers an immediate investigation.
	•	Radiation safety warning signs must be posted on the X-ray room door. The door must be closed and latched during all exposures.

5.4  Teleradiology Workflow
All X-ray films requiring radiologist reporting and all ECGs requiring cardiologist reporting are transmitted via the Sure approved teleradiology channel. No imaging result is released without a specialist report.
	•	Radiographer performs the procedure and produces the digital image (DICOM format via digitiser).
	•	Image is transmitted via the approved, encrypted channel to the contracted radiologist. Do NOT use personal WhatsApp accounts for clinical image transmission.
	•	Log all transmissions in the Teleradiology Dispatch Register: patient name, LIS number, time sent.
	•	Turnaround expectations: Routine = within 12 hours. Urgent = within 2 hours. Radiographer follows up proactively if TAT is not met.
	•	On receipt of report, upload to LabSmart LIS against the patient record.
	•	Notify FDO/CSO that the report is ready. Standard result release protocol then applies.
	•	Physical films are filed in the patient folder and retained for 7 years.

5.5  Image Quality & Reject / Repeat Analysis
	•	Every rejected or repeated image must be logged in the Image Reject Register: date, patient LIS number, body part, reason (positioning, exposure, movement, artefact), and whether a repeat was taken.
	•	Monthly reject rate is calculated: (rejected images / total images) x 100. Target: < 5% per month.
	•	Monthly reject analysis is presented at the Clinical Meeting. Recurring patterns indicate training or equipment issues.

5.6  Result Production & Release (Radiology)
	•	All radiology reports must be produced using LabSmart LIS by the radiographer or sonographer.
	•	ECG results must be reported by the contracted cardiologist. X-ray films must be reported by the contracted radiologist. No ECG or X-ray result is released based on the radiographer's assessment alone.
	•	Radiographers and sonographers may note their technical findings but must not make a diagnostic report without specialist sign-off.
	•	All results must be typed, signed, printed on Sure Report Sheet letterhead, and stamped with the official Sure Diagnostics stamp.

6.3  Chain of Custody Protocol
Every sample that leaves a spoke must be documented before departure. No sample moves between sites without a completed chain of custody.
	•	Lab scientist completes the Sample Transfer Form (STF): patient name, LIS number, tests requested, sample type, collection time, temperature, and any special handling notes.
	•	Samples placed in clearly labelled, sealed biohazard bags with the STF and a receipt copy inside.
	•	Cold chain samples placed in validated cool boxes pre-chilled to 2–8 degrees C (or dry ice for frozen samples). Temperature logged at point of packing.
	•	Dispatch rider signs the Spoke Dispatch Register: date, time, number of samples, cool-box temperature at departure, and destination.
	•	Upon arrival at Hub, receiving scientist signs the Hub Receipt Register: arrival time, packaging condition, temperature on receipt, and any discrepancies noted.
	•	Rejected samples (haemolysed, wrong temperature, incorrect container, unlabelled, or insufficient volume) are logged on the Sample Rejection Form. The originating spoke is notified within 30 minutes.
	•	Rejected samples trigger free recollection at the originating spoke within 2 hours where clinically possible.

Quality Management System (QMS)
Non-conformance, CAPA, audits & continuous improvement
7.1  QMS Philosophy
Sure Diagnostics is committed to producing results that are accurate, reliable, and clinically actionable every time. Our QMS governs every process from sample collection to result release. Quality is not an audit function — it is the way we work. Every staff member is a quality officer.

7.2  Non-Conformance & CAPA System
A Non-Conformance (NC) is any deviation from our documented procedures, quality standards, or expected clinical outcomes. All NCs — regardless of severity — must be recorded, investigated, and resolved.

NC Classification
Definition
Response Time
Minor
Single procedural deviation with no patient impact
Correct within 7 days
Major
Systemic failure or repeated procedural deviation
CAPA within 14 days
Critical
Patient safety risk or near-miss
Immediate escalation; CAPA within 48 hours

CAPA Process:
	•	NC identified — staff member completes Non-Conformance Report (NCR) within 24 hours.
	•	Branch Manager reviews and classifies: Minor, Major, or Critical.
	•	Root cause analysis performed: 5-Why method for Minor; fishbone diagram for Major/Critical.
	•	Corrective action defined with responsible person, resources needed, and completion deadline.
	•	Action implemented and verified by Branch Manager or COO.
	•	NCR closed only after effectiveness is confirmed. Critical NCs reviewed at the monthly Clinical Meeting.

Staff obligations under NDPR:

	•	Patient records on paper must be stored in locked cabinets. Records older than 7 years may only be disposed of by certified destruction.
	•	Any suspected data breach must be reported to the COO within 24 hours.

9.4  Infection Prevention & Control (IPC)
IPC is a patient safety and staff safety obligation. All clinical staff must complete IPC training on commencement of employment and annually thereafter.
Standard Precautions — Apply to ALL Patients, ALL the Time
	•	Hand hygiene: wash with soap and water or use alcohol-based hand rub before and after every patient contact, and after removing gloves.
	•	PPE: gloves for all blood/body fluid contact; lab coat always worn; surgical mask for respiratory or aerosol-generating procedures; eye protection for any splash risk.
	•	Sharps: never recap needles two-handed. Use single-hand scoop or safety device. Dispose immediately into approved yellow sharps bin after each use.
	•	Spills: any blood or body fluid spill must be cleaned immediately with 1% sodium hypochlorite solution.

10.3  Stock Receipt & Storage Protocol
	•	On delivery, the Admin Manager and relevant Head of Unit jointly verify against the Purchase Order: item type, quantity, batch number, expiry date, and condition.
	•	Any discrepancy between delivered goods and the PO is noted on the Goods Received Note (GRN) and reported to the supplier immediately. Payment is not authorised until the discrepancy is resolved.
	•	New stock is entered into SD-IMS under the correct SKU with batch number, expiry date, and quantity received.
	•	Apply FEFO (First Expiry, First Out): newer stock goes to the back of the shelf or fridge. Older stock is always used first.
	•	Cold-chain reagents must be transferred to the appropriate refrigerator within 30 minutes of receipt. Fridge temperature is logged at the time of storage.

10.4  Expiry Management
	•	SD-IMS generates a monthly Expiry Alert Report flagging all items expiring within 60 days. Admin Manager reviews this report by the 5th of each month.
	•	Items within 30 days of expiry with stock exceeding 1 month's usage trigger a vendor return request or inter-branch transfer if another site can use the stock before expiry.
	•	Expired items are quarantined immediately in a labelled 'DO NOT USE' zone and disposed of through certified waste disposal within 7 days.
	•	All expired stock disposals are documented on the Waste Disposal Record and signed by the Branch Manager.

10.5  Monthly Inventory Reconciliation
On the last working day of each month, the Admin Manager conducts a physical count of all reagents and consumables. The count is reconciled against SD-IMS records.
	•	Any variance greater than 5% for a line item requires a written explanation submitted to the COO within 2 working days.
	•	Recurring unexplained variances (two consecutive months) trigger a formal investigation by the COO.
	•	Reconciliation results feed directly into the Branch P&L as the reagent cost of goods sold.

15.2  The Owners Mentality
Every Sure Diagnostics team member is expected to operate with an Owners Mentality. This does not mean you own shares — it means you care about the centre as if you do. You notice what needs to be fixed before being told. You protect the company's resources as if they are your own. You go the extra mile without being asked. You are proud of what we are building.

The behaviours that reflect an Owners Mentality at Sure include:
	•	Reporting facility issues proactively, not waiting to be asked.
	•	Treating every patient with the care you would want for your own family member.
	•	Contributing innovative ideas to improve the business.
	•	Holding yourself and your colleagues to the standards in this manual — not because you are told to, but because you believe in them.
	•	Taking personal responsibility for your results and your growth.
